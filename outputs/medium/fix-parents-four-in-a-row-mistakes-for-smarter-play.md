# Fix Parents' Four in a Row Mistakes for Smarter Play

*By Lina Bautista · Little Smart Genius*

---

![Featured Image](https://littlesmartgenius.com/images/og/fix-parents-four-in-a-row-mistakes-for-smarter-play.jpg)

We’ve all been there. You pull out a classic game like Four in a Row, hoping for a few peaceful minutes, only to have the whole thing fizzle out faster than a sparkler in the rain. Your child loses interest, gets frustrated, or just starts dropping pieces randomly. It feels like a failed attempt at fun, so you tuck the game away, maybe for good. But what if the problem wasn’t the game, but how we’re approaching it?

Lina Bautista, from Little Smart Genius, noticed this pattern too. She saw parents making a few common mistakes that were turning a powerful learning tool into just another forgettable activity. The first slip-up is treating it as a simple time-filler. We hand over the board and check our phones, missing the incredible educational moment happening right in front of us. Four in a Row isn’t just busywork; it’s a **cognitive workout in disguise**. When kids play strategically, they’re building pattern recognition, planning ahead, and learning to anticipate an opponent’s moves—skills that translate directly to math and reading comprehension.

Even with the best intentions, a poor setup can kill engagement before the first piece is played. Printing a brilliant worksheet and using random buttons on a cluttered table just doesn’t cut it. Kids need a dedicated, inviting space to feel like it’s a special event. Lina suggests creating a “game station” with a simple felt mat to dampen sound and a dedicated container of colorful, tactile pieces like pom-poms or large beads. This small act of preparation signals that this time is important and worth their full attention.

Then comes the rule explanation. A rigid, one-size-fits-all rundown of the official rules is a surefire way to frustrate a beginner. Imagine a child’s face falling as they feel blocked and defeated in the first three moves. The key is to adapt. For a young child just starting, try a “Three in a Row” version to make the goal feel achievable. You can even play cooperatively at first, talking through your moves aloud: “Hmm, if I place my yellow pom-pom here, what might you do next?” This models strategic thinking without the pressure of immediate competition.

The magic really happens when we move beyond a single way to play. A game like Four in a Row grows with your child. For a 4-year-old, it might be about color matching and taking turns. For a savvy 9-year-old like Sophia, who Lina mentions, it can become a deep exercise in foresight and handling disappointment. The game offers a safe space to experience a blocked move, take a breath, and formulate a new plan—a lesson in resilience that’s far more valuable than just winning.

So, before you put that game board back in the cupboard, consider this: you might be sitting on an untapped resource for building your child’s brainpower.
